"I propose that fear and the desire for immortality are possible underlying motivations for space colonization... The human obsession with immortality is not new. Stories throughout history have told tales of people searching for immortality and the fountain of youth. When our anthropocentric views become threatened leading to the 'humans must survive' narrative, people react...to protect themselves and their survival. Self-preservation is seen through the need for colonization, religious ideas of an afterlife, or the idea that science will one day hold the key to providing answers to our existential threats. Space X President, Gwynne Shotwell, is one who belieeves we need colonization to survive. The possibility arises that most of the people advocating for colonization are not completely aware of the internal fear and cognitions that drive them. Researchers suggest that '...our goals can be automatically activated by features of the environment, and we then engage in behaviours that satisfy our chronic and temporary goals without being aware of doing so.' As humans, most of us seem to do everthing we can to avoid death. Sociologist Zygmunt Bauman observes that fear of death is a large part of culture, but at the same time, culture tries to hide and shun death. Psychologists Emanuele Castano and Mark Deschesne suggest that, '...death's most terrifying aspect is the cessation of existence, the breaking of a continuity. Belonging to a community and sharing a collective identity guarantees precisely that one's continuity is not lost at death.' Therefore being a part of a group helps to lessen any fears we have about death, such as being a sports fan, a soldier, or even a space community."

- Rayna E Slobodian,

"Selling space colonization and immortality: A psychosocial, anthropological critique of the rush to colonize Mars"
